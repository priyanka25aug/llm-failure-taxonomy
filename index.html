Abstract

Research Summary

Production deployments of large language models reveal failure modes that extend far beyond hallucination. We present a principled, system-level failure taxonomy grounded in 50 real-world incidents across diverse production environments. Our taxonomy defines six failure classes (C1–C6) — spanning factual errors, instruction-following violations, safety failures, context-handling degradation, agentic execution errors, and latent misalignments — together with 88% multi-label classifier accuracy for automated triage.

We introduce a Failure Budget Framework analogous to error budgets in SRE, providing risk-class thresholds and a weighted utilization formula that production teams can operationalize immediately. Our key finding: 50% of production LLM failures are silent — undetectable without purpose-built observability infrastructure.

Taxonomy

Six Failure Classes

Each class is defined by a primary failure mechanism, sub-classes, detectability profile, and a failure budget risk tier. Classes are mutually exclusive at the primary level; multi-label co-occurrence occurs in 38% of incidents.

C1 · Factual

Factual & Semantic Errors

Hallucinated citations, fabricated statistics, wrong numbers, or self-contradictory outputs. The model produces plausible-sounding but incorrect content. Classic hallucination.

C2 · Instruction

Instruction-Following Failures

Format violations, missing required output fields, persona drift, or constraint violations. The model ignores, partially follows, or over-interprets explicit instructions.

C3 · Safety

Safety & Policy Violations

PII leakage, harmful content generation, guardrail bypass, or compliance failures. Includes both over-refusal (false positive) and under-refusal (jailbreak) failure modes.

C4 · Context

Context-Handling Degradation

Long-context memory collapse, recency bias, RAG attribution errors, or contradictory context chunk handling. Failures tied to context window management rather than factual recall.

C5 · Agentic

Agentic Execution Errors

Wrong tool selection, malformed tool arguments, planning loops, premature termination, or irreversible real-world actions taken without adequate verification or human oversight.

C6 · Latent

Latent Misalignment

Sycophantic drift, demographic imbalances in outputs, deceptive compliance, or implicit value steering. Subtle and systematic — only detectable via large-scale output corpus analysis.

Key finding: C3 (Safety) and C5 (Agentic) carry the highest blast radius and are the most frequently silent — 68% of C5 incidents went undetected for more than 24 hours. C2 (Instruction) is the most common class at 22% of all incidents.
Framework

Failure Budget Framework

Analogous to SRE error budgets, each use case is assigned a risk tier (FC-A through FC-D) that defines the maximum weighted failure rate per 1,000 requests.

Severity Weights

Critical × 3 High × 2 Medium × 1 Low × 0.5
Budget Utilization Formula
$U(u,t) = \dfrac{\sum_{e \in E(u,t)} w(e.\text{severity})}{N(u,t)/1000} \times \dfrac{100\%}{B(u)}$
U(u,t) — utilization % for use case u at time t w(s) — severity weight (Critical=3, High=2, Medium=1, Low=0.5) N(u,t) — total requests in window B(u) — budget rate for the assigned FC tier

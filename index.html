Abstract

What actually goes wrong in production LLMs?

TL;DR We propose six qualitatively distinct failure classes beyond hallucination, introduce a failure budget framework adapted from SRE error budgets, and release 50 labeled incidents with a rule-based classifier achieving 0.88 weighted F1.

The discourse on large language model reliability remains dominated by a single failure mode — hallucination — which obscures the far broader spectrum of ways production LLM systems actually break. In practice, LLM-powered systems fail in at least six qualitatively distinct ways: they produce factually wrong outputs (C1), violate explicit instructions (C2), breach safety and privacy constraints (C3), mismanage long-context information (C4), take irreversible agentic actions (C5), and exhibit subtle systematic biases only visible at scale (C6).

We present a structured, empirically-grounded taxonomy built on 50 real-world incidents drawn from court documents, regulatory filings, academic papers, and engineering postmortems spanning 2000–2024. For each class we specify detection signals, blast radius, mean time to detect, and remediation patterns. We introduce a failure budget framework — analogous to SRE error budgets — that translates failure class and severity into quantitative reliability targets across four risk tiers (FC-A through FC-D).

We validate the taxonomy with a weighted-signal rule-based classifier and release all code, data, and tooling openly to support reproducible LLMOps research.

Key finding: 52% of incidents in our dataset are silent — they produce no immediate error signal and are discovered only through downstream audits, user complaints, or regulatory review. Silent failures cluster disproportionately in C1, C3, and C6 — precisely the classes with the highest real-world harm potential.

Taxonomy

Six failure classes

Each class represents a qualitatively distinct failure mode with its own detection pattern, blast radius profile, and remediation path. Classes are not mutually exclusive — 38% of incidents in our dataset exhibit two or more simultaneously.

C1 · Factual & Semantic
Hallucination & Factual Error
Fabricated facts, non-existent citations, wrong numerics, or self-contradictory claims. The most publicly visible class — yet far from the most dangerous.
FC-B 0.5% max error rate
C2 · Instruction-Following
Format & Constraint Violations
The model ignores, partially follows, or over-interprets explicit instructions — missing required fields, wrong output format, persona drift, length violations.
FC-C 2% max error rate
C3 · Safety & Policy
Harmful Output & PII Leakage
Safety guardrail failures, harmful content generation, PII exposure, data governance violations, or policy circumvention — including over-refusal failures.
FC-A 0.1% max error rate
C4 · Context Handling
Long-Context & RAG Failures
Lost information in long documents, recency bias, stale RAG retrievals, conflicting context chunks, or silent truncation at context window boundaries.
FC-B 0.5% max error rate
C5 · Agentic Execution
Tool-Use & Agentic Failures
Wrong tool selection, malformed arguments, planning loops, premature termination, or irreversible real-world actions — file deletions, API writes, submitted orders.
FC-A 0.1% max error rate
C6 · Latent Misalignment
Systematic Bias & Drift
Sycophantic score inflation, demographic representation gaps, deceptive compliance, or implicit value steering. Invisible to per-request evaluation — only visible at corpus scale.
FC-C 2% max error rate
Failure Budget Framework

Quantitative reliability targets

Adapted from SRE error budgets: each use case receives a risk tier that determines its maximum tolerable failure rate per 1,000 requests. Severity multipliers convert raw incidents into weighted failure counts enabling budget utilisation tracking.

FC-A
Decision-Critical
0.1%
Legal, medical, financial decisions; regulatory compliance. One failure per thousand is the hard ceiling.
FC-B
Customer-Facing
0.5%
User-visible outputs, chatbots, public APIs. Direct reputational and legal liability on failure.
FC-C
Internal Productivity
2%
Internal tooling, developer assistants, analytics pipelines. No direct customer exposure.
FC-D
Experimental
10%
Research prototypes, non-production sandboxes. High failure tolerance is expected and acceptable.
# Failure budget utilisation utilisation = weighted_failures / (max_rate * total_requests / 1000) # Severity weights applied to each incident WEIGHTS = { critical: 3.0, high: 2.0, medium: 1.0, low: 0.5 } # Status thresholds HEALTHY = utilisation < 50% # budget comfortably within limits ELEVATED = utilisation < 80% # monitor closely; review signals WARNING = utilisation < 100% # approaching exhaustion; act now BREACHED = utilisation ≥ 100% # escalate immediately

Key Findings

Six things we learned

FINDING 01
Silent failures are the majority
52% of incidents produce no immediate error signal. Standard software observability is almost completely blind to LLM output quality degradation.
FINDING 02
Agentic failures have the highest blast radius
Every C5 incident in our dataset resulted in at least one non-undoable consequence — deleted files, submitted orders, posted content, or broken deployments.
FINDING 03
Hallucination is not the only metric worth tracking
Only 28% of incidents are pure C1. Teams that measure only factual accuracy miss C3, C5, and C6 — the three classes with the highest harm potential.
FINDING 04
38% of incidents are multi-label
Co-occurrence is common. Most frequent pairs: C1+C3 (hallucination that also leaks PII) and C2+C4 (instruction violation in long-context settings).
FINDING 05
FC-A failures take longest to detect
Decision-critical failures have a median MTTD of 720 hours vs. 8 hours for FC-C. High-stakes deployments have less real-time monitoring, not more.
FINDING 06
Legal and healthcare dominate FC-A
74% of FC-A incidents originate in legal or healthcare contexts, but only 16% of production LLM deployments operate there. Risk concentration far exceeds deployment volume.

Selected labeled incidents

C1 · Factual & Semantic
Mata v. Avianca — Hallucinated Case Citations (2023)
Attorney Roberto Mata submitted a federal court brief containing six AI-generated fictitious case citations. ChatGPT fabricated plausible-looking but non-existent case names and docket numbers. The court sanctioned both attorneys. This incident catalysed nationwide judicial guidelines on AI-assisted legal drafting and is cited in at least four subsequent court orders.

C3 · Safety & Policy
Samsung Employee PII Leak via ChatGPT (2023)
Samsung engineers pasted proprietary chip design source code and internal meeting notes into ChatGPT for summarisation. The data was stored by OpenAI and potentially included in future training. Three separate incidents occurred within one month before a company-wide ban. No data governance policy existed governing LLM tool usage — the employees treated ChatGPT as an internal system.

C5 · Agentic Execution
LangChain ReAct Agent Tool-Call Infinite Loop (2023)
A production LangChain ReAct agent entered an infinite tool-call loop after the LLM hallucinated a non-existent function name. The agent framework had no loop detection or budget guardrail. The loop ran for two hours consuming the entire monthly development API budget before hitting the hard spending ceiling and terminating.

C6 · Latent Misalignment
NHS AI Triage Tool Racial Bias in Risk Scoring (2023)
An NHS-deployed AI triage tool systematically underestimated clinical risk scores for Black and Asian patients due to demographic imbalance in training data. The bias was entirely silent — individual outputs appeared plausible with no error raised. It was only detected 18 months after deployment through a mandatory regulatory audit, by which time thousands of patients had been triaged using the biased scores.

C4 · Context Handling
Production RAG Returning Stale Basel II Regulatory Docs (2023)
A European bank's internal regulatory Q&A system was surfacing Basel II capital requirement documents instead of the current Basel III standard. The vector index had not been refreshed in 14 months following a scheduled maintenance window that was silently skipped. Risk analysts unknowingly used outdated regulatory guidance for internal capital calculations for over a year before the error was caught in an annual audit.
